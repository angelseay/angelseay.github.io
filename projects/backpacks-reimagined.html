Backpacks Reimagined

User Interface Design Course, August 2018 - December 2018

üïµÔ∏è Finding a problem

  • Our team was tasked with redesigning an everyday object. We chose the backpack.
  • Although this course focused more on UI in a digital sense, we found that our users didn't see that as a need.
  • We applied common UX processes and principles to our physical design.
  • We brainstormed a list of stakeholders and narrowed it down to college students, commuters, and working professionals.

üë• Researching key stakeholders

  • We brainstormed a list of stakeholders and narrowed it down to college students, commuters, and working professionals.
  • I recorded observations at different locations: on the bus, in the classroom, in our apartments, and during class changes.
  • I helped perform a competitive analysis by looking at news articles, research articles, and Amazon reviews of backpacks.

üëÄ Revealing larger themes

  • We created affinity notes based on user praises and concerns.
  • For notes that were unclear, we decided to seek more information. This was the basis for our interview scripts and survey questions.
  • We settled on three key themes: external structure, what's inside the backpack, and traveling with the backpack.

üìù Iterating on our design criteria

  • We established four design criteria (which would later be modified): Versatility, Safety, Convenience, and Comfort.
  • When we first looked at our user data we simply grouped affinity notes by how similar they were and not on the specific concerns of the user.
  • These new criteria address issues that we found during our user research.

üí≠ Brainstorming backpack designs

  • We used a combination of individual and group sessions to come up with 100 ideas.
  • We clustered the ideas into groups based on what problem they solved. To do that, we first removed any that were unfeasible or didn't solve a relevant backpack issue.
  • We voted on ideas and tried to figure out the best aspects of each, how they fulfilled the design criteria, or if ideas could be combined.

üí° Narrowing it down to 3 ideas

  • We decided to focus on one backpack that would enhance the safety of its user, one that could have fully customizable organization, and one that used technology to conveniently improve little moments in everyday life.
  • I helped design some of the functionality and drew the storyboard for Backpack Night Mode.

üåÉ Backpack Night Mode feedback

Users liked...
  • Potential for feeling safe walking at night
Users were concerned about...
  • Doesn't address accidental calls to police
  • Unclear how the alarm button would be pressed
  • Reflective material may affect the style
Recommendations

Rather than a single press or a long press, the user will now program a pattern when they get the backpack to avoid accidental presses and a separate process for undoing calls.

  • For our final prototype, we listened to feedback from our in-class critique. We settled on three final criteria: Versatility, Individual Safety, and Convenience

üí¨ Heuristic evaluations and focus groups

The Good
  • We found that the lights contributed to not only safety, but also versatility.
  • Participants in our focus groups thought the lights were good for visibility and told us that they would use the lights when they got to their room and it was dark.
  • Potential users also believed that the retractable pocket would be good for placing their keys and accessing smaller items.
  • They thought that the solar charger would be good for when they were traveling.
Current Issues
  • The lights on the backpack can be activated one at a time. However, it is uncommon that the user would need only one light on. Instead of pressing both, a new iteration of the prototype could have a simple switch that would activate/deactivate both lights.
  • More concerns about the silent alarm. We believe we should conduct additional user research to figure out if users prefer the silent alarm over one that makes sound. We still need to figure out the logistics on how the alarm works: if it is a phone call or simply sends a GPS location and who the location is sent to. Is it the nearest police station or 9-1-1 operators or just a friend of the person that owns the backpack? Either way, the alarm will need some sort of battery. It might even be connected to the solar charger.
  • Most of our time was spent developing each stand-alone feature and not how they may interact with each other. We don‚Äôt have clear solutions to these parts of the problem yet, but more discussions with our stakeholders will help us determine if our product fulfills our design criteria.
